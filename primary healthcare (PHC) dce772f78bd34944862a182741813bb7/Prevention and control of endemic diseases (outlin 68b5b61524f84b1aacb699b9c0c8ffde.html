Prevention and control of endemic diseases (outline)

  • basic concepts
    • disease control
      • def

        is a public policy intervention that restricts the circulation of an infectious agent beyond the level that would result from spontaneous, individual behaviours to protect against infection.

      • describes ongoing operationsÂ  aim at reducing:
        1. The incidence of the disease
        1. The duration of the disease and consequent risk of transmission
        1. The effect of the infection including physical and psychosocial complications
        1. The financial burden to the community
      • the agent is allowed to persist at a level where it is not a public health problem and an equilibrium is established between disease agent host and environment.eg Control of Malaria (not eradication)
    • disease elimination
      • def

        reduction to zero of the incidence of a specified disease in a defined geographical area as a result of deliberate efforts.

      • interruption of transmission of the disease
      • Between control and eradication an intermediate goal has been described it is Regional Elimination
      • elimination is now seen as an important precursor of eradication
    • disease eradication
      • def
        • termination of all transmission of infections by extermination of infectious agents
        • vs extinction = the agent doesn't exist in nature or labs
      • termination of all transmission infection by extermination of the infected agent.
      • Only disease eliminated is small pox. Candidates for global eradication include: Polio, Measles, Dracunculiasis.
      • characteristics of eradication
        • It is an absolute process not relative goal.
        • It is all or none phenomenon
        • Eradication is reserved to cession of infection and disease from whole world.
  • monitoring and surveillance
    • monitoring

      It is performance and analysis of routine measurement aimed at detecting changes in environment or health status of population.

      • examples

        monitoring of air pollution, water quality. Also refers to ongoing measurement of performance of health professionals or patientÂ comply to advice.

    • surveillance
      • def
        • to watch over with great attention authority and often with suspicion.
        • continuous scrutiny of the factors that determine the occurrence and distribution of disease and other condition of health
      • examples

        nutritional surveillance, epidemiological and demographic

      • objectives
        • To provide information about & new & changing trends in health status of population e.g. morbidity , mortality, nutritional ,environmental hazards, health practice.
        • To provide feedback which may be expected to modify the policy and system itself
        • To provide timely warning of public health disasters so that intervention may be modified.
  • prevention
    • objective of preventive medicine

      to intercept or oppose the cause and disease processes (in this sense treatment is one mode of intervention).

    • levels of prevention (not the leavell's nomenclature!)
      • primordial
        • It is prevention of development of risk factors when they have not yet appeared
        • Main intervention is through education
        • example

          Hypertension have early origin in childhood for this is the time when life styles are formed (smoking, eating patterns etc)

          Efforts here directedÂ  towards discouraging

      • primary
        • It is action taken prior to onset of the disease which removes the possibility that a disease will ever occur.
        • It is intervention in the pre-pathogenic phase. Accomplish by measures designed to promote general health or to protect against specific disease agents and hazards in the environment.
        • how do you achieve primary prevention
          • health promotion
            • Health education
            • Environmental modifications
            • Nutritional interventions
            • Life style and behavioural changes
          • specific protection
            • Immunization and seroprophylaxis
            • Chemoprophylaxis
            • Use of specific nutrients
            • Protection against occupational hazards
            • Safety of drugs and foods
            • Control of environmental hazards, e.g. air pollution
      • secondary
        • Actions which halt the progress of the disease at its early stage and prevent complications, specifically it is early diagnosis and treatment.
        • For Infectious diseases it's an imperfect tool, more expensive and less effective than Primary Prevention. treat before irreversible changes occur and reverse communicability of infectious diseases
        • for latent diseases, pre-symptomatic Dx and Rx.
      • tertiary
        • interventions in late pathogenic stage and include all measures that reduce or limit impairment and disabilities, minimize sufferings and to promote patient adjustment to irreversible conditions.
        • disability limitation for early symptomatic disease and rehabilitation for late symptomatic disease.
        • Modern Rehabilitation include psychological , vocational and medical components.

      • early diagnosis and treatment
        • def

          Detection of disturbances of homoeostatic and compensatory mechanisms while biochemical, morphological and functional changes are still reversible.

        • importance
          • ToÂ  prevent overt disease, the criteria of diagnosis should be based on early changes before signs appear.
          • This mode is not as effective or economic as primary prevention but is important to reduce mortality and morbidity in certain diseases such as hypertension, cancer of cervix, Breast cancer.
          • For TB, Leprosy and STDs this the only effective mode.
          • For IDs this mode shorten period of communicability and reduce mortality from acute IDs
        • mass treatment
          • Used in control of certain Ds i.e. yaws, pinta, bejel, trachoma and malaria.
          • The rationale is existence of at least 4-5 cases of latent infection for each clinical case.
          • Patient with latent (incubating) infection may develop the D at any time.
          • In this case mass treatmentÂ  is critical factor in interruption of transmission.

        • prevent or halt transition of disease process from impairment to handicap

          • def impairment

            it is any loss or abnormality of psychological, physiological or anatomical structure or function.

            e.g. loss of foot, defective vision or mental retardation. Primary may lead to 2ry (nerve damage lead to ulcer).

          • def disability

            It is any restriction or lack of ability to perform an activity in manner or within the range considered normal for a human being.

          • def handicap

            Disadvantage for a given individual, resulting from impairment or disability that limits or prevent the fulfillment of a role that is normal for that individual.

      • rehab
        • def

          combined and coordinated use of medical , social, educational, and vocational measures for training and retraining the individual to the highest possible level of functional ability

        • It include all measures aimed at reducing the impact of disabling and handicapped conditions and at enabling the disabled and handicapped to achieve social integration.
        • types of rehab
          • medical: restore function
          • vocational: restore capacity to earn living
          • social: restore fam and social relationships
          • psychological: restore dignity and confidence
