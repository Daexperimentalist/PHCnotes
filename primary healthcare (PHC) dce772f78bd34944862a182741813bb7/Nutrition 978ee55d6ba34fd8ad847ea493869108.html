Nutrition

  • define nutrition?

    the science of food and its relation to health. It is the intake of food, considered in relation to the body‚Äôs dietary needs.

  • types of nutrients?

    Nutrients are the complexes contained in food; it is divided into:

    • Macronutrients which form main bulk of food ( i.e. protein, fats and carbohydrates).
    • Micronutrients required in small amounts ( i.e. vitamins and minerals).
  • what's a balanced diet?

    the one that contains a variety of foods in such quantities & proportions that meet all the needs for maintaining health, vitality & general well-being. It has become an accepted means to safeguard a population from nutritional deficiencies.

  • dietary recommendations
    • children
      • infants and children
        • WHO and UNICEF recommendations
          1. Early initiation of breastfeeding within 1 hour of birth;
          1. Exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life;
          1. Breastfeeding should be "on demand", as often as the child wants day and night; and
          1. Bottles or pacifiers should be avoided
          1. Introduction of nutritionally-adequate and safe complementary (solid) foods at 6 months together with continued breastfeeding up to 2 years of age or beyond.
        • breastfeeding
          • what're the benefits?
            • for infants?
              • It is safe and contains antibodies that help protect infants from common childhood illnesses such as: diarrhea and pneumonia, the two primary causes of child mortality worldwide.
              • Infant and young child feeding is a key area to improve child survival and promote healthy growth and development
              • Breast milk is readily available and affordable, which helps to ensure that infants get adequate nutrition.
            • in the long-term ( children)?
              • Breastfeeding contributes to a lifetime of good health.
              • Adolescents and adults who were breastfed as babies are less likely to be overweight or obese.
              • They are less likely to have type-II diabetes and perform better in intelligence tests.
            • for mothers?

              short-term:

              • The risk of postpartum haemorrhage may be reduced by breastfeeding immediately after delivery
              • postpartum depression is less among women who breastfed Also breastfeeding accelerate recovery of pre-pregnancy weight.

              long-term:

              • reduced risk of breast and ovarian cancer
              • exclusive breastfeeding can delay the return of fertility, Mothers who breastfeed exclusively and frequently have less than a 2% risk of becoming pregnant in the first 6 months postpartum, provided that they still have amenorrhoea.
          • what's colostrum?
            • Colostrum is the special milk that is secreted in the first 2‚Äì3 days after delivery. It is produced in small amounts, about 40‚Äì50 ml on the first day, but is all that an infant normally needs at this time.
            • Colostrum is rich in white cells and antibodies & provides important immune protection to an infant when he or she is first exposed to the micro-organisms in the environment.
            • Colostrum contains a larger percentage of protein, minerals and fat-soluble vitamins (A, E and K) than later milk.
          • what're the reasons to avoid giving infants a formula?
            • Infant formula does not contain the antibodies found in breast milk.
            • The long-term benefits of breastfeeding for mothers and children cannot be replicated with infant formula.
            • When infant formula is not properly prepared, there are risks arising from the use of unsafe water and unsterilized equipment or the potential presence of bacteria in powdered formula. Malnutrition can result from over-diluting formula to "stretch" supplies.
            • If formula is used but becomes unavailable, a return to breastfeeding may not be an option due to diminished breast milk production.
          • why is bottle feeding not recommended?
            • Risk of contamination
            • Prone to over-dilution
            • infective diarrhea
            • Costly
            • Asso. w/ infantile obesity or protein-calorie malnutrition
          • HIV and breastfeeding?
            • An HIV-infected mother can pass the infection to her infant during pregnancy, delivery and through breastfeeding.
            • However, antiretroviral (ARV) drugs given to either the mother or HIV-exposed infant reduces the risk of transmission.
            • Together, breastfeeding and ARVs have the potential to significantly improve infants' chances of surviving while remaining HIV uninfected.
            • WHO recommends that when HIV-infected mothers breastfeed, they should receive ARVs and follow WHO guidance for infant feeding.
          • types of mixed feeding?
            • Complemental : bottle is given because mother‚Äôs milk is insufficient
            • Supplemental: bottle is given to replace 1 or more feeding when mother is away for periods longer than feeding intervals

            • Need to be nutritionally-adequate, safe and appropriately fed in order to meet the young child‚Äôs energy and nutrient needs.
            • Start at 6 months of age with small amounts of food, and increase the quantity as the child gets older, while maintaining frequent breastfeeding.

            • Increase the number of times that the child is fed complementary foods as the child gets older and feed a variety of nutrient-rich foods to ensure that all nutrient needs are met.

  • malnutrition
    • def?

      Is defined as a pathological state resulting from a relative or absolute deficiency or excess of one or more essential nutrients

    • what're the causes of malnutrition ( nutritional disorders)?
      • An insufficient intake of food or of certain nutrients.
      • An inability of the body to absorb and use nutrients.
      • Or by over consumption of certain foods.

      • Infectious diseases are important conditioning factors responsible for malnutrition.
      • Diarrhea, measles, malaria, T.B, all contribute to malnutrition.
      • Cultural influences e.g. food habits, customs; believes; traditions and attitudes‚Ä¶etc.
      • Socio economic factors includes poverty, ignorance, large family size lack of knowledge regarding the nutritive value of food.
    • what're the forms of malnutrition?
      1. Under nutrition : results when insufficient food is eaten over an extended period of time
      1. Over nutrition ( Obesity) pathological state resulting from the consumption of excessive quantity of food over an extended period of time.
        • Obesity
          • def?

            abnormal or excessive fat accumulation that presents a risk to health.

            • The fundamental cause of obesity and overweight is an energy imbalance between calories consumed on one hand, and calories expended on the other hand.
          • what's the cause of global fatness?
            1. a global shift in diet towards increased intake of energy-dense foods that are high in fat and sugars but low in vitamins, minerals and other micronutrients
            1. a trend towards decreased physical activity due to the increasingly sedentary nature of many forms of work,
            1. changing modes of transportation, and increasing urbanization
          • how do you measure obesity?
            • using the BMI for +18 ( weight in Kg / height in m squared)
            • 18.5 = underweight
            • 18.5 - 24.9 = healthy weight
            • 25 - 29 = overweight
            • +30 = obese
          • how to prevent people from getting fat?
            • achieve energy balance and a healthy weight;
            • limit energy intake from total fats,
            • increase consumption of fruit and vegetables, as well as whole grains;
            • limit the intake of sugars;
            • increase physical activity at least 30 minutes per day
            • More activity may be required for weight control.
          • what's the double burden of malnutrition?
            • The double burden of malnutrition is a situation where overweight and obesity exist side-by-side with under-nutrition in the same country.
            • We see this a lot in countries that are going through fast economic growth, also known as the nutrition transition.
      1. Imbalance a pathological state resulting from a disproportion among essential nutrients with or without the absolute deficiency of any nutrient.
      1. Specific deficiency the pathological state resulting from a relative or absolute lack of an individual nutrient.
    • what're the common nutritional problems in public health?
      • low birth weight

        (i.e. birth weight less than 2.5 Kg), is a multi factorial problem although not all causes of LBW are known, maternal malnutrition & anemia appear to be significant risk factors. Other associated factors includes, maternal illness, very young age, high parity, close birth intervals‚Ä¶etc.

      • Iodine deficiency
        • It is a major public health problem for populations throughout the world, particularly for pregnant women and young children.
        • The most devastating outcomes of iodine deficiency are increased perinatal mortality and mental retardation
        • The main factor responsible for iodine deficiency is a low dietary supply of iodine .It occurs in populations living in areas where the soil has a low iodine content. Crops grown in this soil, therefore, do not provide adequate amounts of iodine when consumed.
        • When iodine requirements are not met, thyroid hormone synthesis is impaired. If iodine deficiency occurs during the most critical period of brain development (from the foetal stage up to the third month after birth), the resulting thyroid failure will lead to irreversible alterations in brain function.

        • Anaemia is a condition in which the number of red blood cells or their oxygen-carrying capacity is insufficient to meet physiologic needs, which vary by age, sex, altitude, smoking, and pregnancy status.

        • Anaemia is an indicator of both poor nutrition and poor health.
