  • Water
    • water sources and treatment
      • water sources?
        • Rainwater(the prime source of all water.)
        • Surface water (rivers, streams, lakes)
        • Groundwater (from wells and springs).
        • part sinks into the ground to form ground water; part of it evaporates back into the atmosphere & some runs off to form streams & rivers which flow ultimately into the sea. This is known as the <water cycle>.
      • what makes a safe and wholesome water?
        • Free from pathogenic agents,
        • Free from harmful chemical substances,
        • Pleasant to taste, i.e. free from color and odour,
        • Usable for domestic purposes.
      • what's polluted water?

        Water is said to be polluted or contaminated when it does not fulfill the above criteria of safe and wholesome.

        Water quantity is important as water quality.

        • more serious aspect caused by man activity includes?
          • sewage,
          • industrial & trade wastes,
          • agricultural pollutants,
          • Physical pollutants.
      • What's water treatment, and what's the purpose?
        • Water treatment is an essential way to have whole some water.
        • The purpose of water treatment is to remove substances which may be dangerous to human health, & produce safe & wholesome water. Purification of water is either on a large scale, or on a small scale.
      • what determines the water treatment method?
        1. nature of raw water
        1. desired standards of water supply

        e.g. ground water (wells & springs) may need no treatment other than disinfection, while surface water (river water), requires extensive treatment.

      • Types of water purification (treatment)?
        • small scale

          Three methods are generally available for purifying water on domestic scale, they can be used singly or in combination:

          • Boiling
          • Chemical Disinfection
          • Filtration
        • large scale

          the components of a typical purification system comprise one or more of the following:

          • Storage
            • is either in natural or artificial reservoirs, it provides natural purification, about 90% of suspended impurities settle down in 24 hrs by gravity,also a tremendous drop takes place in bacterial count. About 90% drops in the first 5-7 days.
            • The optimum period of storage of river water is considered to be about 10-14 days.
          • Filtration
            • about 98 -99 % of bacteria, are removed by filtration, apart from other impurities. There are 2 types of filters:
            • Slow sand or biological filters,
            • Rapid sand or mechanical filters.
          • Disinfection
            • what's the purpose of disinfection?

              The purpose of disinfection is to kill or inactivate pathogenic (disease causing) microbes. Disinfection of water is therefore an important step in the control of water-borne diseases such as cholera, typhoid, hepatitis A and many diarrheal diseases, although all of these diseases may be transmitted in other ways as well.

            • what're the properties of disinfectants?
              • Should be capable of destroying the pathogenic organisms present
              • Should not leave products of reaction which are toxic or change colour or odour
              • Has a reasonable cost
              • Has the property of leaving residual concentration to deal with any possible recontamination.
            • What's the ideal disinfectant?

              Chlorine; it is a supplement not a substitute to the other steps.

              • what're the principles of chlorination?
                • The mere addition of chlorine to water is not chlorination. There are rules to be followed:
                1. Water should be clear
                1. Chlorine demand of water should be estimated the correct dose to be applied is the sum of chlorine demand of water plus the free residual chlorine of 0.5mg/L.
                1. Contact period at least one hour to kill bacteria & viruses.
              • what's the breaking point?

                The point at which the chlorine demand of water is met is known as “break point”

              • what's super chlorination?
                • A method applicable to heavily polluted waters whose quality fluctuates greatly .
                • It is the addition of large doses of chlorine to water & removal of excess of chlorine after disinfection (Super chlorination followed by de-chlorination)
            • what's a good pretreatment agent?
              • ozone gas
                • It eliminates undesirable odour, taste & colour.
                • It has a strong veridical effect.
                • It inactivates viruses in a matter of seconds unlike chlorine which needs minutes to do so.
                • The drawback of ozone is that after it has done its job it decomposes & disappears, there is no residual germicidal effect.
    • Diseases Related to Water
      • children are a susceptible population.
      • Water-borne Diseases
        • def?

          Caused directly by the presence of an infective agent in the water such as diseases caused by ingestion of water contaminated by:

          1. Human or animal excrement, which contain pathogenic microorganisms e.g. Viral (viral hepatitis A and E, polio etc),Bacterial,Protozoal (amoebiasis, giardiasis),Helmenthic (roundworm, threadworm, hydatid disease etc)
          1. Chemicals that have an adverse effect on health such as: Flouride , Nitrates from fertilizers, Carcinogenic pesticides (DDT),Lead (from pipes).Chemical pollutants may also affect man’s health indirectly by accumulating in aquatic life (e.g. fish) used as human food .

      • Water-washed Diseases
        • Diseases caused by poor personal hygiene and skin and eye contact with contaminated water.
        • These include scabies, trachoma, typhus, and other flea, lice and tick-borne diseases.
      • Water-based Diseases

        Diseases caused due to the presence of an aquatic host (intermediate host) such as: A snail for schistosomasis ,Cyclops (water fleas) for guineaworm (Dracunculiasis)

      • water-related diseases

        Water-related diseases are caused by insect vectors, especially mosquitoes, that breed or feed near water like: dengue, filariasis, malaria, onchocerciasis, trypanosomiasis ….etc

      • Health ED points to control water diseases
        • Hygiene education
        • Improvements in habitation and general sanitation
        • Higher education training in water-related issues.
        • Do not defecate outdoors.
        • Wash hands with soap and water before handling food.
        • Wash, peel or cook all raw vegetables and fruits before eating.

    • sanitation
      • def?

        is any system that improves proper disposal of human and animal wastes, proper use of toilet, and avoiding open space defecation.

      • excreta disposal
        • what's a sanitation barrier?
          • anything that hinders the safe disposal of excreta is important , so that it does not contaminate the environment, water, food or hands.
          • Primary barrier - prevent excreta accumulation and shortcut the disease carrier from the source.
          • secondary barrier - shortcut the carrier from the host.
        • what's a sanitation ladder?

          several different sanitation options (layers) required with varying levels of convenience and cost within a community.

  • pollution
    • air pollution
      • def?

        is contamination of the indoor or outdoor environment by any chemical, physical or biological agent that modifies the natural characteristics of the atmosphere.

      • What are the affects of air pollution?
        • health effects
          • Immediate effects borne by the respiratory system which result in acute bronchitis & if the pollution is intense it may result in immediate death of suffocation.
          • Delayed effects which are linked with air pollution are chronic bronchitis, lung cancer, bronchial asthma, emphysema, & respiratory allergies.
        • social and economic effects

          which includes mainly destruction of plant & animal life.

      • what're modes of air pollution?
        • household air pollution

          smoke, cooking gases, usage of solid fuels

        • ambient (outdoor) air pollution

          Most sources of outdoor air pollution are well beyond the control of individuals and demand action by cities, as well as national and international policymakers in sector like transport, energy waste management, buildings and agriculture.

      • causes of air pollution according to WHO?
        • Particulate matter (PM)

          consists of a complex mixture of solid and liquid particles of organic and inorganic substances suspended in the air. The most health-damaging particles are those with a diameter of 10 microns or less, (≤ PM10), which can penetrate and lodge deep inside the lungs. Chronic exposure to particles contributes to the risk of developing cardiovascular and respiratory diseases, as well as of lung cancer

        • ozone (O3)
        • nitrogen dioxide (NO2) and
        • sulfur dioxide (SO2)
        • Ozone is a major factor in asthma morbidity and mortality, while nitrogen dioxide and sulfur dioxide also can play a role in asthma, bronchial symptoms, lung inflammation and reduced lung function.
      • prevention and control?
        • new cleaner tech
        • Dilution e.g. establishment of green belts btw industrial & residential areas.
        • legislations for cleaner environment

    • radiation
      • types of radiation
        • ionizing radiation is the one which has the ability to penetrate tissues and deposit its energy within them.
        • non ionizing radiation refers to many electro magnetic radiation of wave lengths longer than those of ionizing radiation so have less energy e.g. radio frequency.
      • sources of radiation
        • natural: - cosmic source - environmental [radio active elements e.g. uranium].
        • man made: - x-rays[ patients & radiologists] - nuclear explosions.
      • biological effects of radiation?
        • somatic
          • Immediate effects according to the dose, can only cause mild lassitude or which can be fatal
          • Delayed effects :take time to develop , the latent period may vary from a few weeks to years & the delayed effects are mainly leukaemia, malignant tumours, foetal development abnormalities & shortening of life.
        • genetic

          shows effects in remote off-springs as a result of injury to the chromosomes which are associated with sterility or affects the genes.

      • radiation hazards?
        • Occupational hazards due to ionizing radiation may be :
        • Acute burns, dermatitis and blood dyscarsias
        • Chronic exposure may cause malignancies and genetic effects.
      • preventive measures?
        • Workers must wear a dosimeter which shows accumulated exposure to radiation.
        • Employees should be monitored at intervals not exceeding 6/12.
        • Shielding should be used (lead shielding).
        • Pregnant women should not be allowed to work at places where there is continuous exposure
